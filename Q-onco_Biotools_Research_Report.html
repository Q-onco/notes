18+ libraries torn apart across molecular visualization, microscopy image analysis, AI/ML structure prediction, and genomics. This document is the research foundation for the Q-onco Biotools module — written to be shared with collaborating researchers, discussed in lab meetings, and used as a technical brief for implementation decisions.

This report covers everything needed to make informed decisions about building a browser-native biotools platform for HGSOC research. It is organized as follows: first, a deep dive into the relevant HGSOC biology so the tool choices are grounded in actual research needs. Then a genuine teardown of each tool category — not a surface-level feature comparison but an explanation of why each library is architectured the way it is, what tradeoffs the original authors made, where those tradeoffs hurt us, and where we can do better. The organizing thesis is that browser-native biotools for HGSOC are now achievable — not in principle but in practice, with specific libraries, specific APIs, and a defined implementation path. The compute primitives (WebGL2 for GPU rendering, WebAssembly for near-native compute, ONNX Runtime Web with WebGL Execution Provider for ML inference) are all production-ready in 2026. The gap that remains is the integration layer — which is exactly what Q-onco is positioned to build.

Section 01

HGSOC Biology Deep Dive

homologous recombination, PARP inhibitors, the TME, genomic landscape, and the imaging data types that define the lab workflow. Understanding this biology is what separates our platform from a generic bioinformatics tool.

Homologous Recombination and Why It Matters for Treatment

High-grade serous ovarian cancer is defined, more than any other cancer type, by its relationship to DNA double-strand break (DSB) repair. The homologous recombination (HR) pathway is the cell's high-fidelity mechanism for repairing DSBs — it uses the sister chromatid as a repair template, preserving sequence integrity. In HGSOC, this pathway is defective in approximately 50% of tumors, and that deficiency is the primary basis for PARP inhibitor sensitivity.

The HR pathway works in a defined sequence. When a DSB occurs, the MRN complex (MRE11-RAD50-NBS1) senses the break and recruits ATM kinase. ATM phosphorylates H2AX (γH2AX) across a megabase flanking the break, creating a chromatin mark visible by immunofluorescence as a distinct nuclear focus — this is why γH2AX foci are a standard assay for DSBs. ATM then phosphorylates CHK2, which propagates the checkpoint signal to stop cell cycle progression. BRCA1 is phosphorylated by ATM/CHK2 and localizes to the break site via its RING domain (which forms a heterodimer with BARD1) and its BRCT domain (which recognizes phospho-peptides on CtIP and other resection factors).

End resection — converting the blunt DSB ends to 3′ single-stranded overhangs — requires CtIP (activated by CDK2 phosphorylation), the MRN complex, and EXO1 or DNA2/BLM helicase. BRCA1-BARD1 promotes this resection step; 53BP1, which competes with BRCA1 at break sites, inhibits resection. The balance between BRCA1 and 53BP1 determines whether a break is repaired by HR (high fidelity, uses sister chromatid) or non-homologous end joining (NHEJ, error-prone).

Once ssDNA overhangs are generated, RPA coats them, and then BRCA2 loads RAD51 onto the ssDNA, displacing RPA. BRCA2's BRC repeats (there are 8 of them, encoded in the massive exon 11) each bind one RAD51 monomer and mediate loading onto ssDNA. Once loaded, RAD51 forms a helical nucleoprotein filament that invades the intact sister chromatid, searching for homology. This is the central catalytic step of HR. PALB2 is the bridging protein that physically connects BRCA1 (via coiled-coil interactions) to BRCA2 (via its C-terminal WD40 domain), making PALB2 loss functionally equivalent to BRCA1 or BRCA2 loss for HR purposes.

RAD51 paralogs (RAD51B, RAD51C, RAD51D, XRCC2, XRCC3) stabilize the RAD51 filament and are required for efficient HR. RAD51C and RAD51D mutations occur in ~4-5% of HGSOC and confer PARP inhibitor sensitivity. BARD1 (BRCA1-associated RING domain protein) forms the obligate heterodimer with BRCA1's RING domain — most BRCA1 missense mutations affecting the RING domain disrupt this interaction.

PARP Inhibitor Mechanism: Trapping vs. Catalytic Inhibition

PARP1 (poly-ADP-ribose polymerase 1) is the primary single-strand break (SSB) repair enzyme. When a SSB occurs, PARP1 binds to the break via its two zinc finger domains, activates, and adds ADP-ribose polymers (PAR chains) to itself and nearby histones, recruiting other repair factors. After repair, PARP1 releases from the repaired DNA through auto-PARylation.

PARP inhibitors (PARPi) were originally thought to work purely by blocking PARP1's catalytic activity, causing SSBs to accumulate, collapse replication forks into DSBs, and overwhelm the HR-deficient cell's ability to repair them — synthetic lethality. However, the key mechanism is actually PARP trapping: PARPi lock PARP1 onto damaged DNA. Trapped PARP1-DNA complexes are more cytotoxic than simple SSBs because they physically block replication forks and are extremely difficult to resolve — requiring specialized helicases (FANCJ, HELQ) and nucleases (FAN1) just to dislodge the trapped complex.

This distinction matters enormously for the four clinical PARPi: talazoparib is the most potent PARP trapper (~100× more potent than olaparib in PARP trapping assays), followed by niraparib, then olaparib and rucaparib (roughly equivalent), then veliparib (weakest trapper, essentially catalytic inhibitor only). In clinical practice, talazoparib is used for BRCA1/2-mutant breast cancer; olaparib and niraparib are the primary HGSOC drugs. The trapping potency difference explains why talazoparib can achieve clinical responses at much lower doses but also causes more hematologic toxicity.

For structural biology: the PARP1 catalytic domain (WGR + HD + ART domains) is the drug binding site. Olaparib, niraparib, rucaparib, and veliparib all bind in the NAD+ binding pocket of the ART (ADP-ribosyl transferase) domain. The nicotinamide mimic moiety of each drug occupies the donor site. PDB entries of interest: PARP1 + olaparib (4ZZZ), PARP1 + niraparib (4R6E), PARP1 + rucaparib (5LXE). These are all appropriate structures for docking validation.

PARP Inhibitor Resistance Mechanisms

Resistance to PARPi in HGSOC falls into several mechanistic classes, each requiring different analytical approaches:

  • BRCA1/2 reversion mutations: secondary somatic mutations that restore the reading frame of the original germline frameshift or nonsense mutation. The restored protein may be partially functional, restoring enough HR to survive PARP inhibitor pressure. Detection requires deep sequencing of the original mutation site in ctDNA or tumor biopsy. Frequency: 20-30% of acquired olaparib resistance in BRCA2-mutant HGSOC.
  • 53BP1 / RIF1 / REV7 loss: restores HR-independent repair by allowing end resection even in the absence of BRCA1; tumors become NHEJ-deficient instead of HR-deficient, losing PARPi sensitivity but remaining sensitive to DNA-damaging agents. Detection: IHC for 53BP1 nuclear foci.
  • PARP1 mutations: rare, but PARP1 splice variants that skip the DNA-binding zinc fingers reduce PARP trapping while maintaining catalytic activity; these are detected by RNA-seq of the PARP1 locus.
  • CDK12 bypass: CDK12 loss causes HR deficiency by reducing expression of HR genes (BRCA1, FANCD2, RAD51); resistance can emerge by alternative splicing or upregulation of backup HR factors.
  • Drug efflux: P-glycoprotein (ABCB1) upregulation reduces intracellular PARPi concentration; not a primary mechanism but observed in some cell line models.

HGSOC Genomic Landscape

The TCGA ovarian cancer analysis (2011, n=489) established the canonical molecular portrait of HGSOC:

  • TP53: ~96% of cases carry somatic mutations. This is near-universal, making HGSOC molecularly distinct from almost every other cancer type. Hotspot mutations: R248W (missense, dominant negative), R175H, R273H, R248Q, R249S. Splice site mutations (e.g., intron 4, intron 6 splice donors) are also common. The consequence is p53 protein accumulation detectable by IHC — a diagnostic criterion for HGSOC.
  • BRCA1: ~11% germline or somatic mutation. BRCA1 mutations cluster in a few hotspots: c.5266dupC (5382insC in older nomenclature, exon 20, causes frameshift), c.181T>G (p.C61G, RING domain missense disrupting BARD1 interaction), exon 11 large deletions. BRCA1 promoter methylation adds another ~5% with epigenetic silencing.
  • BRCA2: ~8% germline or somatic. Hotspot: c.6174delT (exon 11 frameshift, common Ashkenazi founder mutation), large deletions in the BRC repeat region.
  • RAD51C, RAD51D, BARD1, PALB2: ~5-7% combined. These are now included in standard germline panel testing for ovarian cancer. RAD51C/D mutations confer PARPi sensitivity equivalent to BRCA2 loss.
  • CDK12: ~3%. CDK12 is a cyclin-dependent kinase that phosphorylates the RNA polymerase II CTD and regulates transcription elongation of long HR genes (BRCA1, FANCD2). Loss causes focal tandem duplication signature and moderate HR deficiency. CDK12-mutant HGSOC is PARPi-sensitive but less so than BRCA1/2-mutant.
  • CCNE1 amplification: ~20%. Cyclin E1 amplification at chr19q12 drives HR-proficient HGSOC — this subgroup does NOT benefit from PARPi but shows sensitivity to CDK2 inhibitors. CCNE1-amplified tumors have distinct genomic stability profiles (low HRD scores) and worse prognosis on platinum.
  • Homologous Recombination Deficiency (HRD) scores: composite genomic scar measures including Loss of Heterozygosity (LOH) — segments >15Mb that are copy-number neutral LOH; Telomeric Allelic Imbalance (TAI) — allelic imbalance extending to telomeres; Large-Scale State Transitions (LST) — breakpoints between segments >10Mb. The Myriad myChoice HRD score (sum of LOH + TAI + LST, threshold ≥42 = HRD-positive) is the clinical companion diagnostic for niraparib. Genomic HRD scars persist even after BRCA reversion mutations restore HR, making them retrospective markers of past HR deficiency.

tumor microenvironment in HGSOC

HGSOC has a complex and clinically significant tumor microenvironment (TME). Unlike many solid tumors, HGSOC frequently spreads to the peritoneal cavity, where it encounters a distinct immune landscape shaped by ascitic fluid and omental stromal cells.

CD8+ tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs): Higher intratumoral CD8+ TIL density is consistently associated with improved overall survival in HGSOC across multiple studies (Zhang et al. 2003, Sato et al. 2005). The correlation holds for both primary and recurrent disease. Quantification is typically done on IHC-stained sections: CD8 antibody on FFPE sections, manual or digital scoring of CD8+ cells per mm² within the tumor epithelial compartment. The challenge is compartment definition — stromal CD8 vs intraepithelial CD8 have different prognostic significance.

PD-L1 expression: PD-L1 (CD274) is expressed on tumor cells (~50% of HGSOC cases), stromal cells, and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs). The scoring system matters: tumor proportion score (TPS, % of tumor cells PD-L1+), combined positive score (CPS, PD-L1+ cells of any type per 100 tumor cells), and area-based scoring. PD-L1 positivity in HGSOC does not reliably predict response to checkpoint inhibitors — multiple Phase II trials of pembrolizumab and atezolizumab showed modest ~10% ORR in unselected HGSOC regardless of PD-L1 status. Combination approaches (PARPi + anti-PD-L1) are in ongoing trials.

Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs): HGSOC has one of the highest TAM densities of any cancer type. TAMs are broadly classified as M1-like (pro-inflammatory, CD86+, HLA-DR-high) or M2-like (anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, CD163+, CD206+). In HGSOC, the TAM population is predominantly M2-like and correlates with worse prognosis. CXCL12 secreted by cancer-associated fibroblasts recruits monocytes that differentiate into M2 TAMs in the ascitic fluid microenvironment. Quantifying the M1:M2 ratio by IHC (CD68 total, CD163 M2-marker) or multiplexed IF is a key research readout.

Cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs): CAFs in HGSOC express α-SMA (ACTA2), FAP, and S100A4. They remodel the extracellular matrix, secrete immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, CXCL12, IL-6), and provide metabolic support to tumor cells. CAF subpopulations (myofibroblastic CAFs vs inflammatory CAFs) have distinct spatial distributions visible by spatial transcriptomics.

Imaging Data Types in the HGSOC Lab

Understanding exactly what data researchers work with is essential for building the right tools:

H&E (Hematoxylin and Eosin)

The standard histology stain: hematoxylin stains nuclei blue-purple, eosin stains cytoplasm and extracellular matrix pink. HGSOC is defined histologically by high-grade nuclear atypia, papillary/slit-like architecture, absence of endometrioid glands. H&E slides are scanned at 20× or 40× magnification producing whole-slide images (WSI) in formats like SVS (Aperio), NDPI (Hamamatsu), MRXS (3DHISTECH), or standardized TIFF. A single 40× WSI at full resolution is 50,000 × 40,000 pixels × 3 channels × 1 byte = ~6GB uncompressed. In practice stored as multi-resolution pyramidal TIFF with JPEG tile compression at ~200-500MB per slide.

IHC (Immunohistochemistry)

Protein expression on FFPE sections using antibody-enzyme-chromogen. The most common HGSOC IHC panel: p53 (mutation status), Ki67 (proliferation index), WT1 (lineage marker), PAX8 (Mullerian origin), ER/PR (for low-grade serous distinction), RAD51 (HR functional assay — RAD51 foci form only in HR-competent cells after DNA damage). IHC images are H&E-like but with one specific protein stained brown (DAB, 3,3'-diaminobenzidine) on a hematoxylin counterstain. The DAB channel must be separated from hematoxylin using color deconvolution before quantification.

Multiplexed Immunofluorescence (mIF)

Multiple proteins labeled simultaneously with spectrally distinct fluorophores. Standard panels in HGSOC TME research: 6-plex (DAPI for nuclei, CK/pan-cytokeratin for tumor cells, CD8 for cytotoxic T cells, CD68 for macrophages, PD-L1 for immune checkpoint, Ki67 for proliferation) or 8-10 plex (adding CD163 for M2 TAMs, α-SMA for CAFs, CD4 for helper T cells, FOXP3 for Tregs). Platforms: Vectra Polaris (PerkinElmer), Phenoptics, CODEX (Akoya), MACSima. Output format: multi-channel TIFF, typically 16-bit per channel, channel count = number of markers + DAPI. Pixel size ~0.5µm at 20×. A 6-channel 20× TMA core (1mm diameter) at 16-bit: ~2MP × 6 channels × 2 bytes ≈ 24MB per core. A full TMA slide with 200 cores is ~4.8GB uncompressed — this is what researchers are working with.

FISH (Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization)

Used primarily for CCNE1 amplification detection (dual-color FISH with CCNE1 and chromosome 19 centromere probes) and BRCA1/2 copy number. Images are 2-4 channel fluorescence, 100× oil immersion, single cell resolution. Signal counting (dots per nucleus) is the readout. Not a primary target for our platform — FISH quantification requires very high magnification and is typically done by pathologists.

TMA (Tissue Microarray)

Arrays of small tissue cores (0.6-1.5mm diameter) from many patients on one slide. A typical HGSOC TMA has 100-300 cores, each representing a separate patient or tumor region. TMA scanning produces a single large image (same formats as WSI) that is then digitally deconstructed into individual core images. H-score or Allred scoring per core is the standard output. The key research workflow: scan → core detection → stain quantification → link scores to clinical data (survival, treatment response).

Platform Implication

The Q-onco biotools platform needs to handle: (1) 16-bit multi-channel TIFF for mIF TMA cores, (2) H&E WSI tiles for morphological assessment, (3) IHC images for DAB quantification with color deconvolution, (4) PDB/mmCIF for protein structures, (5) VCF/BAM for genomic variants. Each has completely different rendering requirements, pixel depth, channel semantics, and quantification logic. No single existing browser library covers all of these — which is precisely why a custom integration layer is needed.

Molecular Visualization: Full Teardown

Mol*, NGL, 3Dmol.js, Speck — why each library makes the architectural choices it does, where those choices pay off, and where they leave gaps that define our build strategy.

The Rendering Performance Problem

Before examining individual libraries, it is worth establishing why molecular visualization is a hard rendering problem. A typical HGSOC-relevant protein like BRCA1 has ~1,863 amino acids and ~14,800 non-hydrogen atoms. A protein complex like BRCA1-BARD1-PALB2 has ~35,000 atoms. A nucleosome-bound BRCA1 fragment might have ~100,000 atoms. Viral capsids used in structural biology reach 10M+ atoms. The renderer needs to draw all of these at 60fps for interactive use.

In a naive implementation, each atom is one object in a 3D scene graph. The CPU must update the transform of each object every frame, sort them by depth for transparency, and issue one draw call per object to the GPU. At 14,800 atoms, that is 14,800 CPU updates + 14,800 GPU draw calls per frame. GPU draw calls have a fixed overhead of ~10-50 microseconds each — at 14,800 draws × 50µs = 740ms just in draw call overhead, completely unusable. The solution that every high-performance molecular renderer eventually converges on is GPU instancing: describe the geometry of one sphere once, upload all atom positions/colors/radii into a GPU buffer, and issue a single instanced draw call. The GPU then renders all atoms in one pass.

🧊
Mol* (MolStar)
molstar.org · MIT · ~2MB gzipped · npm: molstar · Standard viewer at RCSB PDB, PDBe, UniProt

Design History and Motivation

Mol* was initiated in 2018 as a collaboration between David Sehnal (CEITEC, Brno), Alexander Rose (RCSB PDB, UCSD), and the PDBe team at EMBL-EBI, with the explicit goal of replacing Jmol as the reference web-based molecular viewer. Jmol, written in Java and later reimplemented as JSmol (pure JS), was the dominant web viewer for 15 years but had fundamental architectural problems: single-threaded, unable to handle structures above ~50,000 atoms, no GPU rendering, no plugin system, and a scripting language (Jmol script) that was effectively a proprietary DSL requiring significant learning investment.

NGL Viewer (Alexander Rose, 2015) was the first serious attempt at a WebGL-based replacement. NGL used Three.js as its rendering foundation and demonstrated that WebGL could handle protein structures interactively. However, Three.js's scene graph overhead began to appear at ~100,000 atoms, and NGL had no state serialization and no plugin system — limiting its extensibility for institutional use at RCSB.

Mol* was designed from the start around three non-negotiable requirements: (1) handle any structure in the PDB including the largest entries (ribosome 70S is ~250,000 atoms, PBCV-1 virus capsid is ~10M atoms), (2) have a complete plugin system that allows third parties to add custom representations and behaviors without modifying core code, and (3) serialize complete viewer state so sessions can be saved, shared, and reproduced.

The WebGL2 Rendering Pipeline

Mol* does not use Three.js, Babylon.js, or any general-purpose 3D engine. It implements its own WebGL2 rendering pipeline from scratch. This was a deliberate choice: general-purpose engines optimize for the scene graph use case (arbitrary movable objects with individual transforms and materials), which is exactly wrong for molecular rendering. The Mol* rendering architecture:

  • Geometry representation objects: Each visual representation (SpaceFill, Cartoon, BallAndStick, Surface, etc.) produces one or more geometry objects. A geometry object for SpaceFill representation contains a vertex buffer with sphere positions, a color buffer (per-instance RGBA), a radius buffer (per-instance float), and an index buffer. These are uploaded to the GPU once and retained in VRAM.
  • Instanced rendering: A single sphere mesh (low-poly icosphere, ~120 vertices) is defined once. When rendering 14,800 atoms, gl.drawElementsInstanced(gl.TRIANGLES, 360, gl.UNSIGNED_SHORT, 0, 14800) is called once. The vertex shader reads the per-instance position/color/radius from attribute buffers (using gl.ANGLE_instanced_arrays or native WebGL2 instancing) to place and color each sphere. Result: ~0.5ms render time for 14,800 atoms.
  • Shader pipeline: Mol* uses physically-based lighting in its shaders: ambient + diffuse (Lambertian) + specular (Blinn-Phong) components with material parameters (roughness, metallic) per representation. The fragment shader computes sphere surface normals analytically using the ray-sphere intersection technique (the quad billboard approach: draw a quad that faces the camera, compute sphere position from texcoord offset, discard fragments outside sphere radius, compute normal from intersection). This gives perfect sphere silhouettes without subdividing the sphere geometry.
  • Multi-pass rendering: Opaque geometry → transparent geometry (sorted back-to-front) → SSAO (screen-space ambient occlusion, optional) → FXAA anti-aliasing → gamma correction. The SSAO pass uses a depth buffer and samples 32 random points in the hemisphere above each fragment to compute occlusion — this is the "Contact shadows" option in Mol*'s quality settings.

The State Tree Architecture

Mol*'s state is organized as a tree of PluginStateObject nodes. Each node has a type (e.g., Download, ParseCif, Trajectory, Model, Structure, StructureRepresentation3D) and transforms that convert one type to another. A typical state tree for loading BRCA1 from AlphaFold looks like:

Download (url: alphafold.ebi.ac.uk/api/prediction/P38398)
  └─ ParseCif (parses mmCIF text into CIF AST)
       └─ TrajectoryFromMmCif (extracts trajectory from CIF data)
            └─ ModelFromTrajectory (picks model 0)
                 └─ StructureFromModel (builds atom-level Structure object)
                      └─ StructureRepresentation3D
                           ├─ Cartoon (secondary structure ribbons)
                           └─ SpaceFill (atoms, colored by pLDDT score)

This tree is serializable to MOLJ (JSON). Loading a MOLJ file reconstructs this exact tree, re-fetches any remote data, re-parses, and produces identical visual output. The MOLJ format is stable across Mol* versions for common operations, making it suitable for long-term session storage in our GitHub .enc system.

The Plugin/Behavior System

Mol* behaviors are classes that register against the PluginContext and subscribe to state change events. A behavior for HGSOC BRCA1 annotation would:

// register against structure load events
this.subscribeObservable(
  this.plugin.state.data.events.changed,
  async ({ state }) => {
    // find newly loaded structures
    const structures = state.select(
      StateSelection.Generators.ofType(PluginStateObject.Molecule.Structure)
    );
    for (const s of structures) {
      const uniprotId = await lookupUniprotId(s.obj.data);
      if (HGSOC_PROTEINS[uniprotId]) {
        await applyHGSOCAnnotations(this.plugin, s, uniprotId);
      }
    }
  }
);

applyHGSOCAnnotations then adds color-by-domain representations (RING domain = red, BRCT domain = blue, unstructured = gray), adds label overlays at known pathogenic variant positions, and adds a custom panel to the right sidebar with variant classification data from ClinVar.

Performance in Practice

Tested performance figures for Mol* on a mid-range laptop (Intel Iris Xe integrated GPU): BRCA1 AlphaFold structure (1863 residues) in Cartoon mode = 60fps; BRCA1 in SpaceFill mode = 60fps; BRCA1-BARD1-PALB2 complex (~5000 residues) in SpaceFill = 60fps; 70S ribosome (250K atoms) in Cartoon = 45fps; 70S ribosome in SpaceFill = 18fps. The SpaceFill limitation at >100K atoms on integrated GPU is fragment shader bound (many overlapping sphere fragments). Discrete GPUs easily handle 250K atoms at 60fps in SpaceFill.

What Mol* Does Well
  • Handles any PDB structure including viral capsids — custom GPU pipeline not limited by scene graph
  • State serialization (MOLJ) — sessions are reproducible across devices and time
  • Plugin system with stable API — we can add HGSOC behaviors without forking core
  • Sequence viewer synchronized with 3D selection — click residue in sequence → highlighted in 3D
  • Electron density map rendering — essential for validating crystal structures
  • Volume streaming for large density maps — downloads 64³ voxel chunks on demand
  • MIT license, actively maintained by RCSB and PDBe with ~monthly releases
  • TypeScript-native — strong types throughout, good IDE integration
Limitations for Q-onco
  • ~2MB gzipped — significant for a tab that loads lazily; plan for code-split + lazy import
  • Plugin API surface is large and occasionally breaks between minor versions
  • Default UI is designed for PDB power users, not clinical oncology researchers
  • No 16-bit multi-channel TIFF — cannot handle IF microscopy data
  • No PDBQT format — docking output from Vina requires conversion before display
  • MOLJ sessions >1MB become slow to parse; large annotation datasets need separate storage
→ Q-onco Integration Strategy

Use Mol* as a headless PluginContext (no default UI) and build a custom Svelte UI around it. This gives complete layout control. Implement HGSOCAnnotationBehavior as a plugin behavior. Use MOLJ for session persistence into our GitHub .enc store. Tree-shake the Mol* import to exclude PDF export, scripting, and legacy format parsers — this reduces the bundle from ~2MB to ~1.2MB gzipped. Load the Mol* chunk lazily when the Biotools tab is first opened.

NGL Viewer
nglviewer.org · MIT · ~800KB gzipped · npm: ngl · Built for MD trajectories and format breadth

NGL was built by Alexander Rose as his PhD project at UCSF. It uses Three.js as its rendering foundation, which has significant implications. Three.js has an excellent ecosystem and is well-maintained, but its scene graph architecture means that as atom count increases, the CPU overhead of updating the scene graph each frame scales linearly. NGL mitigates this by using Three.js's InstancedMesh class for atom representations — creating one InstancedMesh per representation with N instances — which avoids per-object draw calls. However, Three.js still processes the InstancedMesh through its scene graph update cycle, adding overhead that Mol*'s direct WebGL approach avoids.

NGL's strongest differentiator is format support breadth: PDB, mmCIF, mmTF, SDF, MOL2, MOL, XYZ, GRO (GROMACS), PSF, NAMD, XTC, DCD, TRR, TNG, DMS, MMTF, Cube (quantum chemistry), DSN6, CCP4, MRC (electron density maps). The trajectory format support (XTC, DCD, TRR) is unique — NGL can play MD simulations frame by frame, making it the go-to for molecular dynamics visualization. A TrajectoryPlayer component handles frame interpolation and playback speed.

NGL has no plugin system and no state serialization. Camera state can be captured manually but there is no equivalent of MOLJ. This limits NGL's utility for our session-persistence use case.

Q-onco Role

NGL is not our primary viewer — Mol* wins on performance, plugin system, and state serialization. However, if researchers need to visualize MD trajectories of BRCA1 or PARP1 dynamics (a reasonable future use case), NGL is the correct tool. Consider it a future Phase 3 addition, not Phase 1-2.

⚛
3Dmol.js
3dmol.csb.pitt.edu · BSD-3 · ~600KB · npm: 3dmol · Computational chemistry focus, native PDBQT

3Dmol.js was developed at the Pitt Drug Discovery Institute specifically for computational chemistry workflows — docking pose visualization, small molecule display, and protein-ligand interaction analysis. Its defining feature is native PDBQT format support. PDBQT is AutoDock's format: it extends the standard PDB format with two additional columns for partial atomic charge (from Gasteiger method) and AutoDock atom type (AD4 type system: HD for polar hydrogen, OA for acceptor oxygen, NA for acceptor nitrogen, SA for acceptor sulfur, etc.). Every other web viewer requires conversion from PDBQT to SDF or MOL2 before display. 3Dmol.js parses PDBQT directly, making it the essential docking results viewer.

Internally, 3Dmol.js uses its own WebGL implementation (not Three.js). The GLViewer class manages a WebGL context, camera matrices (view, projection, model), and a list of model objects. Rendering uses a custom forward renderer — no deferred rendering, no SSAO, no PBR. The visual quality is lower than Mol* but sufficient for docking result interpretation.

Mouse interaction uses ray-sphere intersection for atom picking: for each mouse click, the viewer computes a ray from the camera through the clicked screen position, tests intersection against each atom sphere, and selects the nearest hit. This is O(n_atoms) per click — for structures under 50,000 atoms this is imperceptible; for larger structures it can stutter. 3Dmol.js is intentionally designed for drug-sized molecules (200-500 atoms) and drug targets (1000-5000 residues) — it degrades gracefully outside that range.

Surface computation uses a JavaScript port of MSMS (Michel Sanner's Molecular Surface) with spatial hashing for performance. Solvent-excluded surface generation for a typical protein takes 2-5 seconds on the main thread — this is a known limitation. For Q-onco, we will need to move surface computation to a Web Worker to prevent UI blocking.

Speck
github.com/wwwtyro/speck · MIT · ~200KB · AO sphere renderer via software rasterizer + WebGL texture

Speck is an elegant solution to a specific rendering problem: producing ambient occlusion (AO) sphere renders of small molecules at interactive frame rates in a browser, without WebGPU or WebGL compute shaders. AO is the lighting effect where surfaces are darker in crevices and corners (less ambient light reaches them) — it gives molecular models the photorealistic depth that makes them usable in publication figures.

The standard approach to real-time AO (Screen-Space Ambient Occlusion, SSAO) requires sampling the depth buffer at multiple points around each fragment, comparing depths, and accumulating occlusion. At reasonable quality (64 samples), this is expensive enough to limit frame rates on integrated GPUs. For molecules with thousands of atoms, SSAO with accurate sphere geometry is very expensive.

Speck's approach: run a software rasterizer in a Web Worker that computes AO contributions analytically per atom, upload the result as a WebGL RGBA texture, and then in the fragment shader simply look up the pre-computed AO value for each screen pixel. The software rasterizer uses a simplified AO formula: for each atom at position p with radius r, AO contribution at screen position (x,y) is computed by integrating over neighboring atoms within a cutoff radius, estimating the solid angle subtended by each neighbor. The Poisson disk sampling of the hemisphere above the atom surface point gives the directional distribution.

The rendering loop: Worker computes AO for the current view → posts a Float32Array result → main thread uploads to gl.RGBA32F texture → fragment shader reads AO values and multiplies with surface color. For a 100-atom drug molecule, the Worker computation takes ~10ms, enabling ~15fps AO rendering — acceptable for publication figure generation (not interactive rotation at 60fps, but usable).

Input format: Speck expects XYZ format (element x y z per line, one line per atom). Conversion from SDF or MOL2 requires extracting atom coordinates and element symbols — achievable in ~20 lines of JS. For drug molecules from our Vina docking pipeline, we parse the PDBQT output, extract heavy atom coordinates, and pass to Speck.

Q-onco Role

Speck is used for drug molecule display in the PARP inhibitor library panel. When a researcher selects olaparib, niraparib, rucaparib, or another compound from the pre-loaded library, Speck renders it with AO sphere mode. The output looks like a high-quality CGI render suitable for direct inclusion in a grant figure or presentation slide. RDKit.js provides the 3D coordinates from SMILES, Speck handles the rendering.

Napari, QuPath, CellProfiler, Cellpose, StarDist, ilastik — why they're built the way they are, what can actually run in a browser, and the specific algorithms we need to implement.

Why Existing Tools Cannot Be Ported to the Browser

This is a question worth addressing explicitly before discussing individual tools, because the answer is not "they're too big" — it's more specific than that.

  • Napari: Uses VisPy for rendering, which uses PyOpenGL, which wraps native OpenGL calls through CFFI bindings to C libraries. There is no WASM port of PyOpenGL, and VisPy's rendering engine is tightly coupled to the Python object model (NumPy arrays passed by reference). The layer data model is elegant and worth copying architecturally, but the implementation is not portable.
  • QuPath: Built on JavaFX with JVM. Uses OpenSlide (a C library) for reading NDPI/SVS/MRXS formats. The computational pathology algorithms (cell detection, tissue classification) are written as Groovy scripts evaluated by BeanShell. JVM → browser compilation via TeaVM or j2cl exists but produces bloated output (OpenSlide C library would need Emscripten) and has not been attempted for QuPath.
  • CellProfiler: 200,000+ lines of Python, 50+ C extensions (scipy, scikit-image native modules, ITK for 3D image processing). scikit-image has a partial Pyodide port, but the full CellProfiler pipeline engine with its module system and measurement infrastructure is impractical to port.
  • ilastik: The interactive learning core (random forest on hand-crafted features) is the one component that can be ported via ONNX — scikit-learn Random Forest can be exported to ONNX via sklearn2pmml or directly via the ONNX sklearn converter. The forest is typically 100-200 decision trees, each with depth 5-10 — inference is just tree traversal, trivial to implement in JS or run via onnxruntime-web.

The practical conclusion: for cell segmentation (Cellpose, StarDist) we use pre-trained ONNX models. For everything else (Napari, QuPath, CellProfiler), we take architectural inspiration and implement equivalent algorithms from scratch in browser-native code. For the rendering layer, we build our own WebGL2 image viewer.

16-bit TIFF: The Format We Must Understand Deeply

Multi-channel fluorescence microscopy data arrives as 16-bit TIFF. Understanding the format is prerequisite to implementing the decoder:

TIFF (Tagged Image File Format) is a container format with an IFD (Image File Directory) structure. An IFD is a list of tag entries, each specifying: TagID (2 bytes), FieldType (2 bytes, e.g., LONG=4, RATIONAL=5), Count (4 bytes), and Value/Offset (4 bytes — either the value inline or a file offset to the value data). Tags relevant to 16-bit fluorescence microscopy:

  • BitsPerSample (tag 258): for 16-bit data, value is 16; for 8-bit, 8. Crucially, many image display tools (browser Canvas API, CSS) only support 8-bit per channel — rendering 16-bit data requires manual normalization to 8-bit range per channel.
  • SampleFormat (tag 339): value 1 = unsigned integer (most fluorescence data), value 3 = IEEE float (some confocal systems). The decoder must handle both.
  • SamplesPerPixel (tag 277): number of channels per pixel. For a 6-plex mIF stack stored as planar (PlanarConfiguration=2), each channel is stored in a separate IFD. Some systems store all channels interleaved in one IFD (PlanarConfiguration=1), others as separate IFDs per channel per z-slice per time point.
  • PlanarConfiguration (tag 284): 1 = chunky/interleaved (RGBRGBRGB...), 2 = planar (RRRR...GGGG...BBBB...). For multi-channel IF, planar configuration is common — all pixels for channel 1, then all pixels for channel 2, etc.
  • TileWidth/TileLength (tags 322/323) vs StripOffsets (tag 324): large images use tile-based storage (e.g., 256×256 or 512×512 tiles); smaller images use strips. Tile-based access enables random access to image regions without loading the full file — critical for large WSI tiles.
  • ImageDescription (tag 270): in OME-TIFF, this contains an XML document describing the full experiment metadata: channel names (DAPI, CK, CD8, CD68, PD-L1, Ki67), fluorophore wavelengths (405nm, 488nm, 555nm, 647nm, etc.), physical pixel size in µm, z-spacing, time interval, objective NA. Our decoder must parse this XML to label channels correctly.
  • Compression (tag 259): value 1 = uncompressed, 5 = LZW, 8 = Adobe Deflate (zlib), 34892 = JPEG2000 (used by some confocal systems). We need decompression support for all common compression types.

The existing browser libraries: geotiff.js (designed for geospatial rasters, handles 16-bit unsigned and float, tile access, LZW/Deflate/LERC compression) is the closest fit but does not parse OME-TIFF XML metadata. UTIF.js handles basic TIFF parsing but clips 16-bit values to 8-bit on decode. Our custom decoder will use geotiff.js for the low-level TIFF parsing and add OME-XML parsing on top.

The WebGL2 IF Viewer Architecture

Rendering 16-bit multi-channel fluorescence data correctly requires GPU-side normalization. The problem: the WebGL Canvas displays 8-bit-per-channel RGBA. If we normalize 16-bit values to 8-bit on the CPU (divide by 255), we lose the dynamic range that makes fluorescence data meaningful — a Ki67 stain in a tumor cell might read 48,000 counts, while background reads 800 counts; the relevant signal range is instrument- and sample-specific.

The correct approach: upload each channel as a 16-bit texture (gl.R16UI internal format, gl.RED_INTEGER format, gl.UNSIGNED_SHORT type — available in WebGL2). In the fragment shader, normalize using per-channel contrast limits (min/max counts) supplied as uniforms. This allows the researcher to interactively adjust contrast per channel without re-uploading texture data:

// WebGL2 fragment shader for 6-channel IF rendering
uniform usampler2D u_ch[6];     // 6 R16UI textures
uniform vec2 u_limits[6];       // [min, max] per channel
uniform vec4 u_color[6];        // pseudocolor (e.g., blue for DAPI)
uniform bool u_active[6];       // channel on/off toggle
in vec2 v_uv;
out vec4 fragColor;

void main() {
  vec3 rgb = vec3(0.0);
  for (int i = 0; i < 6; i++) {
    if (!u_active[i]) continue;
    float raw = float(texture(u_ch[i], v_uv).r);
    float norm = clamp((raw - u_limits[i].x) /
                       (u_limits[i].y - u_limits[i].x), 0.0, 1.0);
    // gamma correction for perceptual linearity
    norm = pow(norm, 0.45);
    rgb += norm * u_color[i].rgb;
  }
  fragColor = vec4(clamp(rgb, 0.0, 1.0), 1.0);
}

The contrast limits can be set automatically using percentile stretching: sort all pixel values for a channel, find the 1st and 99th percentiles, use those as min/max. This is computed once in a Web Worker when the image loads. The researcher then has sliders in the UI to adjust per-channel min/max, with the GPU updating the display in real time since only uniform values change.

Cellpose
cellpose.readthedocs.io · BSD · ~25MB ONNX · Gradient field U-Net · WebGL EP: ~2-5s per 512×512 image

The Gradient Field Algorithm — Why It Works

Classical cell segmentation uses watershed: find intensity minima (cell centers), grow regions outward until they meet at ridges (cell boundaries), assign each pixel to its nearest minimum. Watershed fails systematically when two cells touch — there is no intensity ridge between them, just a smooth transition, and watershed assigns the boundary pixels to one cell or the other arbitrarily. The error rate on densely packed cells (as in TMA cores or ascites cytospins) makes raw watershed results unreliable.

Cellpose's solution is to reframe the problem: instead of finding boundaries between cells, find the flow field that points every pixel toward its cell's center of mass. Two touching cells have centers in different directions; a pixel at the boundary between them will flow to different centers depending on which cell it belongs to. The segmentation is recovered from the flow, not from boundary detection.

The gradient field G(p) is defined such that following G from any pixel p in the foreground converges to the center of its cell. Cellpose trains a U-Net to predict this gradient field directly from the image. The network is not predicting the mathematically exact gradient of any potential function — it is approximating the concept via supervised learning on ground truth masks (where true centers are known). The training objective is L2 loss between predicted and true gradient vectors, plus cross-entropy loss on the cell probability channel.

U-Net Architecture

The Cellpose U-Net has the following structure (cytoplasm model variant): Input: [1, 2, H, W] — channel 0 is the cytoplasm/cell marker channel, channel 1 is the nucleus marker channel (can be zeros if no nucleus channel available). Encoder: 4 blocks of {Conv2d 3×3, BatchNorm, ReLU, MaxPool2d 2×2}, feature maps 32→64→128→256→512. Bottleneck: 2 Conv2d 3×3 layers at 512 channels. Decoder: 4 blocks of {Bilinear upsample 2×, Concatenate with skip connection, Conv2d 3×3, BatchNorm, ReLU}, feature maps 512→256→128→64→32. Output heads: [H, W, 2] for gradient (X, Y), [H, W, 1] for cell probability. Total parameters: ~24.7M.

Postprocessing: Euler Integration in JavaScript

After the ONNX model runs, the gradient tensor [H, W, 2] and probability tensor [H, W, 1] are available as Float32Arrays. The postprocessing steps that must be implemented in JS:

// Step 1: threshold probability
const fg_mask = prob.map(v => v > 0.5 ? 1 : 0);  // foreground pixels

// Step 2: Euler integration — follow gradient to convergence
const STEPS = 200;
const endpoints = new Float32Array(H * W * 2).fill(0);
for (let y = 0; y < H; y++) {
  for (let x = 0; x < W; x++) {
    if (!fg_mask[y * W + x]) continue;
    let px = x, py = y;
    for (let s = 0; s < STEPS; s++) {
      const yi = Math.round(py), xi = Math.round(px);
      if (yi < 0 || yi >= H || xi < 0 || xi >= W) break;
      px += gradX[yi * W + xi];
      py += gradY[yi * W + xi];
    }
    endpoints[(y * W + x) * 2]     = px;  // convergence x
    endpoints[(y * W + x) * 2 + 1] = py;  // convergence y
  }
}

// Step 3: cluster convergence points (1px radius grouping)
// Each unique cluster = one cell; assign mask ID to all pixels
// that converged to the same cluster center

This is acceptable as a one-time computation that runs while showing a progress indicator. The result is a label mask (same size as input, integer value = cell ID, 0 = background) that is then used for feature extraction.

StarDist
stardist.net · BSD · ~15MB ONNX · Star-convex polygons · 2D_versatile_he for H&E · EPFL/EMBL

Star-Convex Polygon Representation

StarDist's mathematical insight: nuclei in H&E or fluorescence images are approximately star-convex — meaning for any point inside a nucleus, the line segment from that point to the nucleus center lies entirely inside the nucleus. This is true for most cell nuclei (which are roughly ellipsoidal) but not for cells with complex shapes like neutrophils or cells in mitosis.

For a star-convex shape, the boundary can be completely described by the center point (cx, cy) and N radial distances r₁, r₂, ..., rₙ at N equally-spaced angles θₖ = k × (2π/N). StarDist uses N=32, giving 32 radial distances per nucleus. The boundary polygon is: (cx + rₖ × cos(θₖ), cy + rₖ × sin(θₖ)) for k=1..32. This is a vastly more compact representation than a pixel mask (32 floats vs thousands of pixels) and directly produces vector polygons usable in image analysis software.

The 2D_versatile_he Model

This model was trained on a curated dataset of 830 annotated H&E images from 7 public datasets: MoNuSeg (multi-organ, 30 images), DSB2018 nuclear segmentation challenge, TNBC (triple-negative breast cancer), CPM (colorectal cancer), NuCLS (mixed cancer types), CRYONUSEG (cryosectioned tissue), and TissueNet. The training data spans multiple tissue types, staining protocols, scanning platforms, and magnifications (20×, 40×). Heavy augmentation: random rotation/flipping, elastic deformation, color jitter in HSV space, random noise, motion blur. The model generalizes well to H&E tissue not in the training set, including HGSOC omental metastasis.

Postprocessing: Non-Maximum Suppression on Polygons

After the network produces a probability map [H, W, 1] and distance map [H, W, 32], the postprocessing applies: (1) threshold probability at >0.4 to find candidate nucleus centers, (2) for each candidate center, reconstruct the polygon from the 32 predicted distances, (3) non-maximum suppression based on Intersection-over-Union (IoU) of polygon areas — if two detected nuclei have IoU >0.4, keep the one with higher probability and suppress the other. The IoU computation between two star-convex polygons uses the Sutherland-Hodgman polygon clipping algorithm to find their intersection area.

ONNX input: [1, H, W, 1] (single grayscale channel, normalized to zero mean unit variance). Outputs: dist [1, H, W, 32] (32 radial distances per pixel), prob [1, H, W, 1] (nucleus probability per pixel). The model runs via onnxruntime-web with WebGL EP at ~1-2 seconds per 512×512 patch.

Q-onco Role

StarDist is used for nucleus detection in H&E-stained HGSOC tissue — both for diagnostic grading (nuclear atypia assessment) and for IHC quantification (count Ki67+ nuclei, p53+ nuclei). For the TMA workflow: run StarDist on the H&E/hematoxylin channel → get nucleus polygons → overlay on DAB channel → compute mean DAB intensity per nucleus → classify 0/1+/2+/3+ → H-score.

QuPath's H-Score Algorithm (Reference Implementation)

QuPath is the gold-standard tool for H-score computation in clinical research. Understanding its algorithm is necessary to replicate it correctly in our browser implementation. The H-score computation pipeline:

  1. Color deconvolution: Separate the H&E or IHC image into individual stain channels using the Ruifrok-Johnston algorithm. For H-DAB: optical density OD_R = -log10(R/255), OD_G = -log10(G/255), OD_B = -log10(B/255). The stain vectors for hematoxylin (0.644, 0.717, 0.267) and DAB (0.268, 0.570, 0.776) are used to solve OD = V × S where V is the stain matrix and S is the stain concentrations per pixel. The result: H channel intensity per pixel (nuclear staining) and DAB channel intensity per pixel (protein of interest).
  2. Cell detection: QuPath applies a watershed on the hematoxylin channel to detect nuclei. Optionally, Cellpose can be used instead (QuPath 0.4+ has native Cellpose integration via subprocess).
  3. Per-cell DAB quantification: For each detected cell, compute the mean DAB optical density within the nucleus region (and optionally cytoplasm region as a separate measurement). QuPath also computes cell area, nucleus-to-cell ratio, circularity, and other morphological features.
  4. Intensity classification: Threshold mean DAB OD: 0 = <0.2 OD (negative), 1+ = 0.2-0.4 OD (weak), 2+ = 0.4-0.6 OD (moderate), 3+ = >0.6 OD (strong). Thresholds are researcher-adjustable and stain-dependent — RAD51 IHC uses different thresholds than Ki67.
  5. H-score: (% 1+ × 1) + (% 2+ × 2) + (% 3+ × 3), range 0-300. Allred score: proportion score (0=none, 1=<1%, 2=1-10%, 3=10-33%, 4=33-67%, 5=>67%) + intensity score (0=none, 1=weak, 2=moderate, 3=strong), range 0-8.

Our browser implementation replicates steps 1-5. Color deconvolution is a matrix multiply implementable in a WebGL2 fragment shader (one pass, GPU-accelerated). Cell detection uses StarDist (H&E) or Cellpose (IF). Per-cell intensity measurement is a reduction over polygon interior pixels, done in JS after getting masks from ONNX. H-score computation is trivial arithmetic. The full pipeline for a 1mm TMA core at 20× (~2000×2000 pixels) should complete in under 30 seconds in the browser.

AlphaFold2 — EBI API

What AlphaFold2 Actually Does

AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., Nature 2021) predicts protein 3D structure from amino acid sequence with accuracy competitive with experimental X-ray crystallography for many domains. The architecture has two major components: the Evoformer and the Structure Module.

The Evoformer processes two types of input representations simultaneously: the MSA (Multiple Sequence Alignment) representation — a tensor of shape [N_sequences, N_residues, 256] encoding evolutionary information from homologous sequences — and the pair representation — a tensor of shape [N_residues, N_residues, 128] encoding pairwise relationships between all residue pairs. The model searches genetic databases (UniRef90, BFD, MGnify) and retrieves up to 512 homologous sequences aligned to the query. These homologs constrain the structure: residue pairs that co-evolve (correlated mutations across species) are almost always in contact in the 3D structure (mutual information = distance constraint).

48 Evoformer blocks alternate between MSA row-wise attention (for each residue pair, attend across all homologous sequences), MSA column-wise attention (for each sequence, attend across residues), and triangular updates of the pair representation (message passing over triangles of residue triples). The triangular update enforces geometric consistency — if residue A is close to B, and B is close to C, then A and C must be in a consistent geometric relationship.

The Structure Module takes the pair representation and produces 3D backbone frames for each residue. Each frame is a rigid body (rotation + translation in 3D space). The key innovation is Invariant Point Attention (IPA): an attention mechanism that operates on both the 1D sequence features and the 3D spatial coordinates simultaneously, and is equivariant to global rigid-body transformations (rotating the whole protein doesn't change the predicted structure). 8 IPA blocks iteratively refine the backbone frames. The final step predicts side-chain torsion angles (χ1-χ4) from the sequence and refined backbone, and places side-chain atoms geometrically.

Interpreting pLDDT and PAE for HGSOC Proteins

pLDDT (predicted Local Distance Difference Test) is a per-residue confidence score from 0-100. It is not a direct error estimate but correlates strongly with experimental LDDT scores. Practical interpretation: >90 = very high confidence, rigid domain; 70-90 = good, likely correct backbone; 50-70 = low confidence, may be disordered or have alternate conformations; <50 = very low, likely intrinsically disordered region (IDR). HGSOC implications: BRCA1's RING domain (residues ~1-100) has pLDDT >90 and is well-structured. The large central region (~300-1600) is mostly IDR with pLDDT <50 — the AlphaFold structure in this region should not be taken as a reliable 3D model, though it is correctly flagged by the pLDDT coloring. The BRCT domain (1640-1863) has pLDDT 80-90.

PAE (Predicted Aligned Error) is a matrix of shape [N_residues × N_residues] where PAE[i][j] is the expected position error for residue j's Cα atom when residue i's frame is used as the reference frame. Low PAE between two domains means the model is confident about their relative orientation (they are likely in physical contact or rigidly connected). High PAE between domains means the model is uncertain about their relative orientation (they may be connected by a flexible linker or the interaction may not be predicted). For BRCA1: the PAE matrix shows low values within the RING domain, within the BRCT domain, but high values between RING and BRCT — reflecting the ~1500-residue flexible linker separating them. For BRCA1-BARD1 heterodimer prediction in AlphaFold-Multimer, low PAE between the two RING domains indicates the heterodimer interface is reliably predicted.

ESM-2 (Evolutionary Scale Modeling)
Meta AI · MIT · 150M param model · ~600MB ONNX · ~30ms/residue on WebGL EP

How Protein Language Models Work

ESM-2 (Lin et al., Science 2023) is a transformer language model trained on 250 million protein sequences from UniRef90 using masked language modeling. The training objective: randomly mask 15% of amino acid tokens in a sequence, train the model to predict the masked residues from the surrounding context. This forces the model to learn the statistical regularities of protein sequences — which amino acids co-occur, which substitutions are tolerated, which are deleterious.

The architecture is standard BERT: N layers of transformer encoder blocks, each with multi-head self-attention and a feed-forward network. The 150M model has 33 transformer layers, 640 hidden dimension, 20 attention heads, context length up to 1022 residues. The 650M model (33 layers, 1280 dim) and 3B model (36 layers, 2560 dim) give better variant effect predictions but are too large for browser caching (1.2GB and 5.3GB ONNX respectively). The 150M model at ~600MB is the practical maximum for IndexedDB storage.

Log-Likelihood Ratio for Variant Scoring

For a missense variant at position i (wild-type amino acid WT, mutant amino acid MUT), the ESM-2 log-likelihood ratio (LLR) is:

LLR(i, WT→MUT) = log P(MUT | sequence with position i masked) − log P(WT | sequence with position i masked)

Negative LLR means the model assigns lower probability to the mutant than the wild-type — the mutation is "unusual" in evolutionary context, which correlates with pathogenicity. Published benchmarks (Fraternali et al. 2023, Notin et al. 2022): ESM-2 150M achieves AUC ~0.83-0.87 on ClinVar BRCA1 pathogenic vs benign classification, Spearman correlation 0.44-0.52 with deep mutational scanning (DMS) experimental data for individual proteins. This is comparable to PolyPhen-2 and SIFT, without requiring homology databases or conservation scores — ESM-2 derives everything from the learned sequence statistics.

BRCA1-specific threshold: based on calibration against ClinVar pathogenic variants, LLR < −2.5 at the BRCA1 RING domain corresponds to ~85% precision for pathogenic classification. The BRCT domain requires different thresholds (~LLR < −2.0) due to different evolutionary constraint. These thresholds should be displayed to the researcher with uncertainty ranges, not as binary calls.

  masked[position] = '';

  // Tokenize: ESM-2 vocabulary: 20 AAs + special tokens
  const tokens = tokenize(masked);  // Int32Array, shape [1, seq_len+2]

  // Run inference — ~30ms/residue on WebGL EP
  const { logits } = await session.run({
    input_ids: new ort.Tensor('int32', tokens, [1, tokens.length])
  });
  // logits shape: [1, seq_len+2, 33]  (33 = vocab size)

  const posLogits = logits.data.slice(
    (position + 1) * 33, (position + 2) * 33
  );  // logits at masked position
  const log_probs = softmax(posLogits).map(Math.log);

  return log_probs[AA_INDEX[mutAA]] - log_probs[AA_INDEX[wtAA]];
}

For a comprehensive BRCA1 pathogenicity landscape (paper figure), we can batch positions (20 positions per batch) and run overnight as a background task.

The Vina Scoring Function

AutoDock Vina uses an empirical scoring function derived from the X-Score function (Ouyang et al. 2013), trained on a set of known protein-ligand complexes with measured binding affinities. The score is a weighted sum of five interaction terms computed over all protein-ligand atom pairs within 8Å:

Score = w₁ × gauss₁ + w₂ × gauss₂ + w₃ × repulsion + w₄ × hydrophobic + w₅ × hydrogen_bond + w₆ × N_tors
  • gauss₁(d): e^(-(d/0.5)²) — short-range attraction, peaks at atom surface contact (d=0)
  • gauss₂(d): e^(-((d-3)/2)²) — longer-range attraction, models van der Waals beyond contact
  • repulsion(d): d² if d < 0, 0 otherwise — steric clash penalty for atom overlap
  • hydrophobic(d): 1 if d < 0.5, 0 if d > 1.5, linear interpolation between — applies only to C-C and other nonpolar pairs
  • hydrogen_bond(d): ramp from 0 (d>0) to -1 (d≤-0.7) — applies to donor-acceptor pairs
  • N_tors: number of rotatable bonds in ligand × 0.058 — entropic penalty

where d = distance between atom surfaces = (r_ij − vdW_radius_i − vdW_radius_j). The weights (w₁=-0.035579, w₂=-0.005156, w₃=0.840245, w₄=-0.035069, w₅=-0.587439) are derived from PLS regression on experimental binding affinities.

Global Search: Monte Carlo + BFGS

Vina's docking algorithm uses an iterated local search with Monte Carlo global sampling. Each MC step perturbs the ligand's rigid-body degrees of freedom (3 translational DOF: x,y,z; 3 rotational DOF: quaternion) and torsional DOF (one angle per rotatable bond). A perturbation is accepted if the score improves or with Metropolis probability exp(-ΔScore/T) at temperature T. After each accepted MC step, BFGS (Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno) local minimization refines the conformation. This combination finds low-energy minima efficiently while escaping local traps via MC.

The exhaustiveness parameter controls the number of independent MC searches run in parallel (default 8; higher = more thorough but slower). In WASM, threads are replaced by sequential runs (no pthreads support in WASM without SharedArrayBuffer). For Q-onco, we run exhaustiveness=8 sequentially, which takes ~60-90 seconds for a typical drug-sized ligand (~40 heavy atoms, 6-8 rotatable bonds) against PARP1.

Genomics Browser: IGV.js

How IGV.js handles gigabyte-scale genomic files in a browser via HTTP range requests, what BGZF compression enables, and how we configure it for HGSOC somatic mutation visualization.

The Core Technical Problem

The human genome reference (hg38) is 3.1GB. A whole-genome sequencing BAM file for a single patient is 50-100GB. A typical HGSOC study might have 50-200 patient BAM files. None of these can be loaded into a browser. The solution: indexed file formats and HTTP range requests.

HTTP range requests (RFC 7233) allow a client to request a specific byte range from a file: Range: bytes=1234567-2345678. The server responds with HTTP 206 Partial Content and only the requested bytes. For this to work for genomic data, the file format must be structured such that genomic regions map to specific byte ranges — i.e., the file must be indexed.

BAM and BGZF Format in Depth

BAM (Binary Alignment Map) is the binary encoding of SAM (Sequence Alignment Map), compressed with BGZF (Blocked GNU Zip Format). BGZF is a specialization of gzip: the file is divided into independent blocks, each a valid gzip stream, with a maximum block size of 65,535 compressed bytes. Each BGZF block contains a gzip extra field with the block size (2 bytes, tag "BC"), allowing software to find block boundaries. Between blocks, a random-access index (.bai) maps genomic coordinates to block offsets.

A BAM record (one aligned read) has this binary structure: block_size (4 bytes), refID (4 bytes — reference sequence index), pos (4 bytes — 0-based leftmost position), l_read_name (1 byte — length of read name), mapq (1 byte — mapping quality 0-255), bin (2 bytes — bin in the BAI index), n_cigar_op (2 bytes), flag (2 bytes — bitfield: paired, reverse, duplicate, etc.), l_seq (4 bytes — read length), sequence (4-bit encoded, 2 bases per byte: A=1, C=2, G=4, T=8, N=15), quality scores (1 byte per base, Phred+33), optional tags.

The .bai index uses a bin-based scheme: each chromosome is divided into overlapping bins at multiple resolutions (roughly 512MB, 64MB, 8MB, 1MB, 128KB, 16KB per bin). For a given genomic range [start, end], the overlapping bins can be computed analytically using the bin numbering formula (reg2bins in the SAMtools codebase). Each bin entry in the .bai file contains the BGZF virtual file offsets (64-bit: upper 48 bits = block file offset, lower 16 bits = offset within decompressed block) for reads covering that bin. IGV.js fetches the .bai file once on load (~2-5MB), uses it to compute the byte ranges for the current view, and issues HTTP Range requests for only those BGZF blocks.

IGV.js Architecture

IGV.js implements the browser side of this protocol: (1) load .bai or .tbi (tabix) index, (2) parse bin structure to find file offsets for current view, (3) issue HTTP Range requests via fetch(url, {headers: {Range: 'bytes=start-end'}}), (4) decompress BGZF blocks using pako.js (a JS port of zlib) or the native DecompressionStream API (Web Streams API, available in modern browsers), (5) parse the decompressed BAM/VCF records, (6) render to a Canvas element.

The rendering pipeline per track: a getFeatures(chr, start, end) call returns features for the current view region. These features are rendered to an OffscreenCanvas using 2D Canvas API (arc for reads, rect for blocks, text for labels). The OffscreenCanvas is transferred to the main canvas via drawImage. At 10,000+ reads in view, IGV.js switches from read-level rendering (individual colored rectangles per read) to coverage-level rendering (a histogram of depth per genomic position) to avoid performance issues.

For local files (researcher dragging their own BAM or VCF from their laptop), IGV.js uses the File System Access API: showOpenFilePicker() → FileSystemFileHandle → file.slice(start, end). This exposes the same random-access byte-range interface as HTTP range requests, using local disk I/O. The 100GB BAM file never leaves the researcher's machine — only the needed BGZF blocks for the current view window are read from disk and decoded in memory.

CORS Configuration

For remote BAM files (not local), the file host must send CORS headers: Access-Control-Allow-Origin: * and Access-Control-Allow-Headers: Range. S3, Google Cloud Storage, and Azure Blob all support this via bucket-level CORS configuration. Most institutional HPC storage and FTP servers do not — researchers must either use local file access or set up a compliant file server. This is a real practical limitation for clinical genomic data hosted on hospital systems.

Why Tools Are Built the Way They Are

Five architectural patterns that explain every major design decision across the 18 tools we analyzed — and what they tell us about building correctly from the start.

Pattern 1: The Performance Ceiling of Abstraction

Every layer of abstraction between your code and the GPU or CPU costs performance. The question is not whether to pay the cost, but where the ceiling is and whether it matters for your use case. General-purpose 3D engines (Three.js, Babylon.js, Unity WebGL) put a scene graph between your code and WebGL. The scene graph enables features (object hierarchies, material inheritance, frustum culling) that are valuable for game development and visualization of scenes with <10,000 distinct objects. Above that threshold, scene graph traversal overhead dominates, and you are paying for infrastructure designed for a different problem.

Mol* concluded that the scene graph overhead was unacceptable for molecular rendering and wrote a custom WebGL2 renderer. Cellpose's authors concluded that implementing the Euler integration postprocessing in Python was unacceptable for interactive use and moved it to Cython/CUDA. IGV.js concluded that general-purpose canvas drawing APIs were too slow for read-level alignment display and implemented a custom rendering pipeline with pre-computed per-track bitmaps.

For Q-onco, the pattern implies: use Three.js for nothing that has a performance requirement. Use WebGL2 directly (or via a thin wrapper like regl that exposes instancing without a scene graph) for the molecular renderer and IF viewer. Use Canvas2D API only for UI elements that change infrequently.

Pattern 2: The Main Thread is Sacred

Browser rendering happens on the main thread. Any JavaScript computation that runs for more than ~16ms (one frame at 60fps) will cause a dropped frame. Computations over ~100ms cause perceptible UI freezes. All scientific visualization tools learned this lesson the hard way — early versions blocked the UI during file parsing, and user complaints forced the move to Web Workers.

The Web Worker architecture for scientific computation:

// Main thread: send work, receive results

  type: 'segment',
  imageData: tileBuffer,       // SharedArrayBuffer — zero-copy transfer
  width: 512, height: 512
}, [tileBuffer]);               // transfer ownership to worker

worker.onmessage = ({ data }) => {
  if (data.type === 'progress') updateProgressBar(data.progress);
  if (data.type === 'result')   applyMasks(data.masks);
};

// Worker: compute without blocking UI

  const { imageData, width, height } = data;
  // ONNX inference
  const { gradX, gradY, prob } = await runONNX(imageData, width, height);
  self.postMessage({ type: 'progress', progress: 0.5 });
  // Euler integration
  const masks = eulerIntegration(gradX, gradY, prob, width, height);
  self.postMessage({ type: 'result', masks });
};

SharedArrayBuffer enables zero-copy transfer of typed arrays between the main thread and workers. Instead of copying a 512×512×2-byte image (~500KB), the memory is shared and both threads access the same buffer. Requires COOP/COEP headers on the server response: Cross-Origin-Opener-Policy: same-origin and Cross-Origin-Embedder-Policy: require-corp.

Pattern 3: State Serialization is not a Feature, It is Infrastructure

Every scientific tool that survives more than two years eventually needs to save and restore application state. Mol* invested heavily in this with MOLJ/MOLX. QuPath session files. CellProfiler project files. ilastik .ilp project files. IGV session XML. In every case, the teams that built serialization early (Mol*, QuPath) produce research that is reproducible and shareable. Teams that deferred it (NGL, 3Dmol.js) have users who lose work and cannot share viewer configurations.

Pattern 4: Format Parsers Are Infrastructure, Not Features

Every scientific format has edge cases accumulated over decades of real-world use: optional fields that some software writes and others don't, version-specific quirks, degenerate inputs that violate the spec, and compression variants. Writing format parsers from scratch is high-risk and time-consuming. Every team that did it learned this.

The correct strategy: reuse existing, battle-tested parsers wherever they exist. The tools with the longest community lifespans (Mol*, Napari, IGV.js, QuPath) all have formal plugin/extension systems. Mol*'s plugin system uses constructor-based dependency injection: behaviors declare their dependencies in the constructor, the plugin context resolves them. For Q-onco's custom IF viewer and molecular renderer, we design in extensibility from day one: a LayerRegistry for new image layer types, a RepresentationRegistry for new molecular representations, and a AnalysisRegistry for new quantification methods. When Dr. Amritha's lab gets a new multiplex platform with 12 channels, adding a new channel preset is adding one object to the registry, not modifying core code.

WebGL2 · WASM · ONNX Internals

What is actually happening inside the compute stack — GPU buffer layouts, WASM memory model, ONNX operator dispatch, SharedArrayBuffer zero-copy patterns.

WebGL2 Rendering Pipeline

WebGL2 is a JavaScript API exposing OpenGL ES 3.0 functionality. Understanding the pipeline is necessary for building any custom renderer. A WebGL2 frame proceeds as:

  1. Vertex fetch: The vertex shader is invoked once per vertex (or per instance for instanced rendering). It reads per-vertex attributes from Vertex Buffer Objects (VBOs) bound in a Vertex Array Object (VAO). For molecular rendering: position (vec3), color (vec4), and radius (float) are per-instance attributes; sphere mesh vertex coordinates are per-vertex.
  2. Vertex shader: Transforms vertex position from model space → world space → clip space using the MVP (Model-View-Projection) matrix. Computes the billboard quad size from radius and distance to camera. Outputs interpolated values (varyings) to the fragment shader.
  3. Rasterization: The GPU converts triangles to pixel fragments, interpolating varyings across the surface. For a sphere billboard quad, this generates many fragments — only those within the actual sphere radius will survive discard in the fragment shader.
  4. Fragment shader: For each fragment, computes the final color. For sphere rendering: (1) compute ray-sphere intersection using the fragment's 2D position relative to the sphere center, (2) if no intersection, discard the fragment, (3) compute the surface normal at the hit point, (4) compute lighting (ambient + diffuse + specular) using the normal and light direction, (5) output RGBA color.
  5. Depth test and blending: The depth buffer stores the closest fragment depth at each pixel. Fragments failing the depth test are discarded. Transparent fragments are blended using the blend equation. For molecular rendering: opaque geometry first (depth write enabled), then transparent surfaces (depth write disabled, blend enabled).

For 16-bit IF image rendering: the vertex shader draws a full-screen quad (2 triangles covering the viewport). The fragment shader reads from 6 usampler2D textures (one per channel, R16UI format), normalizes each channel using per-channel min/max uniforms, applies pseudocolor LUTs (vec4 per channel), and additively blends channels with saturation clamping. All computation happens per-pixel on the GPU — updating contrast limits only requires updating uniform values, not re-uploading texture data. The display updates instantly.

WebAssembly Memory Model

WebAssembly (WASM) modules execute in a linear memory space. All memory — stack, heap, global data — lives in one contiguous ArrayBuffer (or SharedArrayBuffer if shared memory is enabled). The WASM module exports: memory (the memory object), functions (compiled from C++ or Rust), and global variables. JavaScript can read and write to WASM memory via typed array views: const heap = new Uint8Array(wasmModule.memory.buffer).

Passing a large array from JavaScript to a WASM function: (1) allocate space in WASM heap using an exported allocator (e.g., malloc compiled into the WASM), (2) copy data from the JS typed array into the WASM heap using a typed array view, (3) call the WASM function with the pointer as an argument, (4) read results from the WASM heap. For Vina: the receptor PDBQT string and ligand PDBQT string are written into WASM heap memory, the dock function operates entirely within WASM memory, and the result PDBQT string is read back out.

With SharedArrayBuffer, JS and WASM share memory: const shared = new SharedArrayBuffer(size); Module.instance.exports.memory.grow(shared) is not the mechanism — instead, the WASM module is initialized with a SharedArrayBuffer as its initial memory, and both JS workers and the WASM module access the same memory without copying.

ONNX Runtime Web: WebGL Execution Provider

ONNX Runtime Web (ort-web) is Microsoft's browser-native ML inference engine. When you specify executionProviders: ['webgl'], the WebGL Execution Provider (EP) is activated. This EP implements ONNX operators (Conv2d, BatchNorm, ReLU, MaxPool, MatMul, etc.) as WebGL2 shader programs, using textures as tensor storage and draw calls as compute dispatches.

Tensor storage in WebGL EP: each tensor is stored as one or more 2D WebGL textures. A float32 tensor of shape [1, 32, 512, 512] (one batch, 32 channels, 512×512 spatial) is stored as: 32 textures of size 512×512, each with RGBA32F format (4 floats per texel — but only using 1 channel per texel for simplicity, or packing 4 feature maps per texel for efficiency). The packing strategy is an implementation detail that affects performance.

Operator dispatch: a Conv2d with 32 input channels → 64 output channels, 3×3 kernel is implemented as a custom GLSL shader that reads from input textures, computes the convolution (as a sum over 32 input channels × 3×3 kernel positions = 288 multiply-add operations per output element), and writes to output textures. The shader is compiled once and cached, then dispatched via gl.drawArrays (a full-screen quad renders the operation to the output texture framebuffer). For a 512×512 feature map: 512×512 = 262,144 shader invocations, each performing 288 multiply-adds = 75M floating-point operations per Conv2d layer. On a mid-range integrated GPU running at ~10 GFLOPS for texture-bound operations, one Conv2d takes ~7-8ms.

Float16 vs float32: the WebGL EP defaults to float32 because not all GPUs support float16 texture writes (EXT_color_buffer_half_float extension). Float16 would halve memory bandwidth but requires hardware support verification. For Q-onco, we test for the extension and fall back gracefully.

The COOP/COEP Header Requirement

SharedArrayBuffer was disabled in all browsers after the Spectre vulnerability was disclosed in 2018, because SAB enables timing attacks via shared memory. It was re-enabled in 2021 with a requirement that pages be "cross-origin isolated" — indicated by two response headers:

Cross-Origin-Opener-Policy: same-origin
Cross-Origin-Embedder-Policy: require-corp

These headers tell the browser that the page does not share its memory space with cross-origin windows (COOP) and that all cross-origin resources loaded by the page must either opt-in to cross-origin sharing via CORP/CORS headers or be loaded from the same origin (COEP). The practical implication for Q-onco: our Vite dev server and production deployment must send these headers. For Vite dev: add headers: { 'Cross-Origin-Opener-Policy': 'same-origin', 'Cross-Origin-Embedder-Policy': 'require-corp' } to vite.config.ts server options. For GitHub Pages production: GitHub Pages does not support custom headers — we need Cloudflare as a proxy (free tier) or a Netlify _headers file if deployed there.

External resources loaded by the page (AlphaFold API responses, CDN-hosted ONNX models) must respond with Cross-Origin-Resource-Policy: cross-origin or Access-Control-Allow-Origin: *. AlphaFold EBI API already sends CORS headers. Our CDN-hosted ONNX model files must be configured to send CORP headers.

The Roadmap & Technical Decisions

Phase-by-phase build plan with the specific technical decisions that must be made at each stage, dependency ordering, and realistic effort estimates based on the tool analyses in the preceding sections.

Technical Decisions Before Writing a Line of Code

Several architectural decisions must be made upfront because changing them later is expensive:

Decision 1: Mol* Integration Mode — Full UI vs Headless

Mol* can be used in two modes. Full UI mode: use the pre-built PluginContext with the default Mol* UI (left panel, sequence viewer, control panel). Pro: faster integration. Con: the Mol* UI is designed for PDB power users and clashes visually with Q-onco's dark Svelte design. Buttons and panels cannot be restyled. Headless mode: instantiate PluginContext directly, attach it to a bare canvas element, and build the UI entirely in Svelte. Pro: complete control over layout and styling. Con: requires learning the Mol* plugin API more deeply, ~3 extra days of work. Recommendation: headless mode. The extra days are worth it to have a unified UI. RCSB's own future viewers use headless mode for the same reason.

Decision 2: ONNX Model Delivery

Three options: (A) Bundle models in the app (bad — 600MB+ models in a PWA bundle is impractical), (B) Download on first use from CDN, cache in IndexedDB (good — one-time download, then offline), (C) Server-side inference (bad — requires always-online, privacy concerns for patient data, server cost). Recommendation: option B with a model version manifest. We maintain a JSON manifest at /models/manifest.json listing all model names, versions, URLs, and SHA-256 hashes. On first load, the app checks the manifest, compares against cached versions in IndexedDB, and downloads only stale or missing models. Total download: Cellpose (~25MB) + StarDist (~15MB) + ESM-2 (~600MB) = ~640MB, downloaded once. After that, the biotools module works offline.

Decision 3: Custom IF Viewer — Build vs geotiff.js

geotiff.js handles TIFF parsing but outputs decoded pixel data as Float32 or Uint16 arrays — not rendered images. We still need to write the WebGL2 rendering code regardless. The question is whether to use geotiff.js for the decode step or write our own decoder. Recommendation: use geotiff.js for decoding (it handles LZW, Deflate, JPEG, PackBits compression, tile/strip reading, and big-endian byte order) and write only the OME-XML metadata parser and the WebGL2 rendering layer. This saves ~2 weeks of development and handles format quirks we haven't encountered yet.

Decision 4: Session Storage Encryption

Session JSON contains annotation data and possibly pixel-level cell mask data, which may be associated with patient samples. We use AES-256-GCM encryption with a key derived from the researcher's GitHub personal access token using PBKDF2 (100,000 iterations, SHA-256, 32-byte output). The salt is stored in the session file alongside the encrypted data. The GitHub PAT never leaves the browser — it is used only as key material for PBKDF2. This means if the researcher loses their PAT, session files cannot be decrypted (acceptable — they would also lose access to the GitHub repo). The implementation uses the browser's native SubtleCrypto API — no external crypto library needed.

Researcher Pain Points

Four detailed workflow scenarios showing the before/after state. Current tools, current time cost, friction points — and how Q-onco biotools changes the workflow.

Pain Point 1: Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS) Interpretation

Scenario: A patient with HGSOC is found to carry BRCA1 p.L1764P (c.5291T>C) — a missense variant in the BRCT domain. The clinical genetic counselor asks the lab whether this is likely pathogenic (would support PARPi treatment) or benign (no treatment implication).

Current Workflow (~2-3 hours)

  • Search ClinVar for p.L1764P → not yet classified (VUS)
  • Search LOVD BRCA1 database → three submissions, two uncertain, one conflicting
  • Run PolyPhen-2 via web form → wait 15 min → "probably damaging" (score 0.923) but this is known to over-predict for BRCT domain
  • Run SIFT via web form → "deleterious" (score 0.02)
  • Open PyMOL or Chimera locally (requires installed software), load BRCA1 BRCT structure (PDB 1T29), manually navigate to residue 1764, check local environment
  • Search literature for functional studies of BRCT domain missense variants → three papers found, none test this specific variant
  • Write up VUS assessment: "Predicted damaging by PolyPhen-2 and SIFT. Position L1764 is in the BRCT2 domain; leucine is conserved across 12 species reviewed. No functional data available. Classification: VUS-likely pathogenic." Time: ~2-3 hours including literature search

Q-onco Workflow (~15 minutes)

  • Type "BRCA1" in Q-onco Biotools → AlphaFold structure loads in Mol* with pLDDT coloring and ClinVar pathogenic variant highlights
  • Click on residue 1764 in the sequence viewer → 3D view zooms to BRCT domain, L1764 highlighted
  • Sidebar shows: "L1764 — BRCT2 domain, alpha-helix. ClinVar: 3 VUS submissions. ESM-2 score: −3.4 (likely deleterious, 88th percentile harmful for BRCT domain)."
  • Click "Functional context" → shows known BRCT missense variant pathogenicity landscape, color-coded by ClinVar status — L1764P appears in a region where multiple known pathogenic variants cluster (T1700A, R1699W, M1775R)
  • Click "Enzo synthesize" → Enzo searches the Q-onco literature database for BRCT domain missense studies, drafts a 3-paragraph VUS assessment using ESM-2 score, structural context, and literature evidence
  • Copy VUS assessment to clinical report. Time: ~15 minutes

Pain Point 2: TMA IHC Scoring for a Biomarker Cohort

Scenario: Lab has IHC-stained 200-core HGSOC TMA for RAD51 protein expression (marker of HR function). Need to score all cores and correlate with clinical data (PFS on PARPi treatment).

Current Workflow (~3-4 weeks)

  • Core facility scans TMA slide → 2-3 day turnaround → ~3GB SVS file
  • Download to lab HPC (slow, requires VPN access)
  • Install QuPath (Java install, setup, version compatibility issues)
  • Import SVS, manually define core boundaries for all 200 cores (~4 hours)
  • Run QuPath cell detection with default settings → poor results on HGSOC staining → tune parameters (nucleus detection thresholds, background correction) → re-run → 4-5 iterations over 2 days
  • Export per-cell DAB measurements to CSV
  • Manually review and exclude artifact cores (out-of-focus, folded tissue, edge artifacts) → 2-3 hours
  • Compute H-score in Excel per core → link to clinical data → statistical analysis
  • Total: 3-4 weeks from scan to data table, with significant QuPath expertise required

Q-onco Workflow (~4-6 hours)

  • Upload TMA TIFF to Q-onco Microscopy module (or connect to OMERO if institution has it)
  • Auto core detection: Q-onco detects TMA array geometry, identifies 200 core positions, shows preview — researcher confirms or adjusts 2-3 boundary-case cores (~10 min)
  • Run StarDist for nucleus detection on the hematoxylin channel across all cores (~20 min, ONNX WebGL EP)
  • Run color deconvolution (WebGL shader, instant) to extract DAB channel per core
  • Compute mean DAB intensity per nucleus, auto-threshold into 0/1+/2+/3+ bins, compute H-score per core — all instant
  • Q-onco shows quality thumbnails for each core; researcher flags 8 artifact cores by clicking (5 min)
  • Export: CSV with core ID, H-score, Allred score, cell count, % positive cells, mean intensity — ready for survival analysis in R or Python
  • Total: 4-6 hours from image upload to analysis-ready data table

Pain Point 3: Multiplexed IF TME Characterization

Scenario: 6-plex mIF staining (DAPI/CK/CD8/CD68/PD-L1/Ki67) on HGSOC primary tumor cores. Need: CD8+ TIL density per mm², PD-L1 H-score on tumor cells (CK+) vs stroma (CK-), CD68+ macrophage density, Ki67+ proliferation index in tumor compartment.

Current Workflow (~1-2 weeks per cohort)

  • PerkinElmer Phenoptics outputs 16-bit multi-channel TIFF per core
  • Load in Phenoptics inForm software (expensive license ~$15K/year, requires Windows)
  • Train tissue segmentation classifier: annotate tumor vs stroma regions manually (2-3 hours training data), run classifier
  • Train cell segmentation: annotate cell boundaries in DAPI channel (1-2 hours)
  • Define phenotype rules: CK+CD8- = tumor, CK-CD8+ = cytotoxic T cell, etc.
  • Run batch analysis on all cores (~3 hours compute for 200 cores)
  • Export per-cell data tables, aggregate in R
  • Total: 1-2 weeks, requires inForm license, Windows, and expert user

Q-onco Workflow (~3-5 hours)

  • Upload 6-channel TIFF → Q-onco IF viewer shows all channels, auto-detects OME-XML labels (DAPI, CK, CD8, CD68, PD-L1, Ki67)
  • Toggle channels on/off, adjust per-channel contrast until image looks correct (5 min)
  • Run Cellpose on DAPI channel → nuclei segmented → run again with CK channel included for cytoplasm → full cell masks with nuclear + cytoplasmic compartments (~5 min)
  • Cell phenotyping: Q-onco presents simple threshold sliders per channel: "CK+ threshold: [slider]", "CD8+ threshold: [slider]" — researcher sets based on positive control cores (15 min)
  • Q-onco computes per-cell phenotype, spatial density (cells/mm²), compartment-specific H-scores, cell-cell distances (CD8 to nearest tumor cell), all instantly from the masks
  • Output: comprehensive CSV + visualization layer overlays on the IF image → export figures directly
  • Total: 3-5 hours, no specialized software license, runs offline in browser

Pain Point 4: Understanding PARP Inhibitor Resistance — Structural Analysis

Scenario: Patient progresses on olaparib after 18 months. cfDNA sequencing shows PARP1 E988K mutation (not previously reported). Lab wants to understand if this mutation affects olaparib binding and could explain resistance.

Current Workflow (~1-2 days)

  • Search literature for PARP1 E988K → no publications found
  • Download PARP1 + olaparib crystal structure from PDB (4ZZZ)
  • Open in PyMOL, navigate to E988, visually inspect — E988 is in the HD domain, ~12Å from the NAD+ binding pocket
  • Manually introduce the K mutation using PyMOL mutagenesis wizard
  • Run AutoDock Vina locally (requires installation, receptor preparation with AutoDock Tools GUI)
  • Prepare olaparib PDBQT from SMILES using external tools
  • Run docking → 30 min → inspect results in PyMOL
  • Compare olaparib binding affinity WT vs E988K → -9.2 vs -8.7 kcal/mol (modest reduction)
  • Write up: "E988K causes modest reduction in predicted olaparib affinity (ΔΔG ~0.5 kcal/mol), may affect PARP trapping rather than catalytic inhibition. Suggest functional assay." Time: 1-2 days

Q-onco Workflow (~45 minutes)

  • Q-onco Biotools → type "PARP1" → AlphaFold structure loads. AlphaFold does not have the olaparib-bound conformation → switch to PDB search → load 4ZZZ (PARP1 + olaparib co-crystal, 2.1Å)
  • Mol* shows olaparib in the NAD+ pocket with HGSOC annotation overlay highlighting known drug-resistance mutations
  • E988K is not in the pre-annotated list → researcher clicks E988 in sequence viewer → sidebar shows "E988: HD domain, 12Å from nicotinamide binding site. Not in ClinVar. ESM-2 score: −1.2 (tolerated, 35th percentile — consistent with resistance mutation rather than loss-of-function)"
  • Click "Dock olaparib" → Vina WASM runs WT receptor (pre-prepared 4ZZZ receptor stored as static asset) → result in ~60 seconds → affinity −9.2 kcal/mol, top pose matches co-crystal structure (validates the pipeline)
  • Click "Mutate E988K" → Q-onco generates the E988K mutant receptor PDBQT (side-chain substitution, energy minimization via OpenBabel) → re-dock → result in ~90 seconds → affinity −8.6 kcal/mol
  • PLIP-lite shows interaction changes: E988K loses a water bridge to the nicotinamide moiety, gains a salt bridge to D877. The binding mode is minimally perturbed — consistent with PARP trapping reduction rather than complete drug displacement.
  • Enzo: "PARP1 E988K reduces predicted olaparib affinity by ~0.6 kcal/mol, consistent with partial resistance. The HD domain mutation may affect allosteric PARP trapping rather than competitive catalytic inhibition. Similar modest-effect mutations (E988A in cell lines) have been associated with 3-5× reduced sensitivity to olaparib without complete resistance. Recommend: measure PAR formation after olaparib treatment in patient-derived cells (if available), and consider dose escalation or switch to talazoparib (higher trapping potency)."
  • Time: ~45 minutes including Enzo synthesis

Build vs Embed: Decision Framework

Every tool, every component — with specific reasoning. Not "it's complex" but exactly why, and what we gain by each decision.

The Decision Rule

Embed when: a library with a permissive license covers ≥90% of our requirements, and the remaining 10% can be added via its extension mechanism (plugin API, behavior system, track registry). Build custom when: no library handles our exact requirements, or the library is fundamentally architectured for a different problem space (desktop, Python, Java), or the domain specificity (HGSOC annotations, mIF quantification) makes general tools structurally inadequate.

The common failure mode: building custom too eagerly (reinventing Mol*'s instanced renderer) or embedding too optimistically (assuming geotiff.js handles OME-TIFF metadata — it does not). The teardowns in sections 02-05 are specifically designed to avoid both failure modes.

Decisions

Protein Structure Visualization → EMBED Mol* (headless)

Why embed: Mol* already has instanced WebGL2 rendering handling 1M+ atoms, a complete plugin system, MOLJ state serialization, sequence viewer, 30+ format parsers, electron density rendering, and is MIT licensed. Building an equivalent renderer would take 3-6 months and would not be as battle-tested. Why headless: the default Mol* UI clashes with Q-onco's design and cannot be restyled. Headless mode adds 3 days but gives complete layout control. What we build on top: HGSOCAnnotationBehavior plugin (auto-highlights pathogenic variants, domain boundaries, PARP inhibitor binding sites), custom Svelte sidebar showing ESM-2 scores and ClinVar data, MOLJ integration with GitHub .enc session storage.

Docking Visualization → EMBED 3Dmol.js

Why 3Dmol.js specifically: native PDBQT format support. Vina WASM outputs PDBQT. Every other viewer requires PDBQT → SDF/MOL2 conversion (adding OpenBabel as another pipeline step). 3Dmol.js eliminates this. Used only for the docking results panel — not as the primary structure viewer. Mol* handles everything else.

Small Molecule AO Rendering → EMBED Speck

Why Speck: ambient occlusion sphere renders look like publication-quality figures. 200KB. The AO computation approach (software rasterizer in Web Worker + WebGL texture) is clever and correct. We cannot replicate the visual quality with standard WebGL without real-time SSAO, which costs more on the GPU. Speck is used only for drug molecule display in the PARP inhibitor library — not for protein structures. Input comes from RDKit.js 3D conformer generation.

Cell Segmentation → EMBED Cellpose ONNX (+ custom JS postprocessing)

Why Cellpose: the gradient field approach outperforms classical watershed for densely packed cells in HGSOC TMA cores and mIF images. Pre-trained cyto2 and nuclei models cover our use cases without fine-tuning. 25MB ONNX model, runs on WebGL EP. What we build: the Euler integration postprocessing (gradient flow → cell mask) does not exist as a browser-native library — it is ~150 lines of JS that we write ourselves. Also: the tiling strategy for images larger than 512×512, the ONNX model download/cache manager, and the mask → measurement pipeline.

Nucleus Detection → EMBED StarDist ONNX (+ custom JS NMS)

Why StarDist: the 2D_versatile_he model was specifically trained on H&E pathology images. Star-convex polygon output is better than pixel masks for IHC scoring (polygon directly defines cell boundary for intensity measurement). What we build: the polygon NMS postprocessing (~100 lines), Sutherland-Hodgman polygon clipping for IoU computation, and the polygon → measurement pipeline (DAB intensity per polygon interior).

Molecular Docking → EMBED Vina WASM + OpenBabel WASM + RDKit.js

Why this stack: full AutoDock Vina scoring function and optimization at 70-80% native speed, no server cost, no data upload. Pre-processing (SMILES → 3D conformer: RDKit.js; format conversion → PDBQT: OpenBabel WASM) is equally necessary and has no alternatives. Complexity acknowledgment: this is the highest-risk Phase 2 item. WASM interop between three libraries, receptor preparation, grid box definition for HGSOC proteins — all require careful implementation. Plan for 6 development days, not 3.

Variant Effect Prediction → EMBED ESM-2 150M ONNX

Why ESM-2: state-of-art protein language model, AUC ~0.85 on BRCA1 pathogenic/benign classification, free, MIT licensed. 150M model is the practical maximum for browser caching (~600MB IndexedDB). Alternative considered: EVE (Evolutionary model of Variant Effect) — requires database of homologous sequences per protein, large download per protein, complex preprocessing. ESM-2 is simpler: tokenize → forward pass → log-probability. Limitation disclosed: 600MB download on first use is significant. Show clear progress indicator and explain the one-time nature. Offer a "compute VUS score" button rather than computing on every variant view.

Genome Browser → EMBED IGV.js

Why IGV.js: production-grade, handles all genomic formats (BAM/VCF/BED/bigWig/seg/MAF), HTTP range requests for remote large files, local file drag-drop via File System Access API, used by GDC and cBioPortal. MIT licensed. The only reasonable choice. What we add: pre-packaged HGSOC annotation tracks (ClinVar HGSOC genes, TCGA OV hotspots, CCNE1 amp region), and the cross-tool link (variant click → Mol* structure + ESM-2 score).

Multi-channel IF Viewer → BUILD CUSTOM (WebGL2)

Why build: no existing browser library handles 16-bit multi-channel fluorescence TIFF with: per-channel contrast controls, pseudocolor LUT per channel, channel on/off toggling, OME-TIFF metadata parsing for channel names, GPU-side normalization. geotiff.js handles decoding but not display. The Canvas2D API handles 8-bit — useless for 16-bit data. OpenSeadragon (WSI viewer) handles 8-bit pyramidal TIFF — cannot extend to 16-bit multi-channel. This is genuinely a domain where no browser library exists, and the implementation is within reach (WebGL2 R16UI textures + custom fragment shader = ~500 lines of code).

HGSOC Annotation Database → BUILD CUSTOM

Why build: this is the most uniquely valuable thing we do. A curated database of HGSOC-relevant proteins with domain boundaries (RING 1-109, coiled-coil 1391-1424, BRCT 1642-1863 for BRCA1), known pathogenic variants with ClinVar classification and publication evidence, PARP inhibitor binding site definitions, functional domain annotations from UniProt features — none of this exists in a pre-packaged form targeted at HGSOC research. We build it as a static JSON file bundled with the PWA, updated monthly via a GitHub Action that queries ClinVar and UniProt APIs.

Protein-Ligand Interaction Profiling → BUILD PLIP-lite in JS

Why not Python PLIP: requires a Python server (cost, complexity, always-online). Why buildable: PLIP's core geometry (H-bond distance/angle, hydrophobic cutoff, pi-stacking centroid/normal) is ~800 lines of Python math that translates directly to JS. For PARP inhibitor interactions specifically, we need: H-bonds to Gly863/Ser904/Tyr907 in the nicotinamide pocket, hydrophobic contacts to Ala898/Leu769/Ile895, and pi-stacking with Tyr907. These interaction types (H-bond, hydrophobic, pi-stack) are the three simplest PLIP routines. Full PLIP parity is not the goal — 90% coverage of drug-protein interactions for HGSOC relevant compounds is achievable in ~800 lines of JS.
